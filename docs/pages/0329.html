Depois, tomando-se o mês da primavera como início do ano, o bissexto foi transferido para o tempo da primavera, porque também antes da primavera se estabeleceu a intercalação do mês lunar mais próximo. Pois o Bissexto deles era inserido entre o primeiro e o segundo de abril: o que era a Tekupha de Nisan. Posteriormente, aceita a forma juliana, o Bissexto começou a ser intercalado entre 25 e 26 de março, razão esta que até hoje vigora entre eles. E porque de 25 de março até 2 de abril, que era a sede do antigo bissexto, restam 7 dias, por isso outros tantos dias hoje se subtraem para investigar a Tekupha de Nisan, assim como se subtraem treze sempre que se investiga a Tekupha de Tisri. Chamam Tekupha o quadrante do ano juliano, que consta de 91 dias e 7 ½ horas. Nos tempos de Moisés, dizia-se que havia apenas uma única Tekupha, como ponto interposto entre o fim do ano antecedente e o início do subsequente: isto no outono — em torno desse tempo, o mais próximo possível, Deus ordena que se celebre [σκηνοπηγίαν] "a festa dos tabernáculos". É, portanto, a Tekupha um momento ou ponto de tempo em que, terminado um quadrante do ano, imediatamente começa outro quadrante. Observam esse ponto tão supersticiosamente que, se o excedessem sequer pela largura de um cabelo, pensariam estar se expondo a grande risco de vida. Pois atribuem a cada Tekupha seu próprio elemento. Por exemplo, à Tekupha de Tamuz, a solstícial, atribuem o fogo. Julgam que, do excesso dos elementos, pende perigo sobre as coisas: por exemplo, da Tekupha de Tamuz [ἐκπύρωσιν] "uma conflagração": e quem no momento daquela Tekupha bebesse ou comesse, seria acometido de febre ardentíssima. Pois se após a divisão restar uma unidade, a Tekupha de Tisri é 9. 0; se dois, a Tekupha de Tisri é 3. 0. E assim por diante. English

the intercalary [day], which we call Bissextile, was inserted in that place where the embolismic lunar month is also inserted, namely before Tishri, although it was not called Tishri but the first month. Then, once the spring month was taken as the head of the year, the bissextile was transferred to springtime, because before spring also the intercalation of the nearest lunar month was established. But after they received from the Seleucids the form of the year and the Callippic lunar period, nothing was changed in the reckoning of the solar year. For their Bissextile was inserted between the first and second of April: which was the Tekupha of Nisan. Afterwards, once the Julian form was adopted, the Bissextile began to be intercalated between the 25th and 26th of March, which method has prevailed among them to this day. And because from March 25 to April 2, which was the seat of the ancient bissextile, there remain 7 days, for this reason the same number of days is today subtracted to investigate the Tekupha of Nisan, just as thirteen are subtracted whenever the Tekupha of Tishri is investigated. They call Tekupha the quarter of the Julian year, which consists of 91 days and 7 ½ hours. In the times of Moses, only a single Tekupha was spoken of, as a point interposed between the end of the preceding year and the head of the following: namely in autumn — around which time, as closely as possible, God commands [σκηνοπηγίαν] "the feast of tabernacles" to be celebrated. The Tekupha, therefore, is a moment, or point of time, at which, when one quarter of the year is finished, another quarter immediately begins. They observe this point so superstitiously that, were they to exceed it by even the breadth of a hair, they would think themselves to be entering upon great danger of life. For to each Tekupha they assign its own element. For example, to the Tekupha of Tammuz, the solstitial one, they attribute fire. From the excess of the elements they suppose danger to threaten things: for example, from the Tekupha of Tammuz [ἐκπύρωσιν] "a conflagration": and whoever should drink or eat at the moment of that Tekupha would be seized by a most burning fever. I have here appended a Table of all the Tekuphas of one quadrennium: which, once the quadrennium is completed, return in a cycle. By dividing the Jewish year by four, you will easily know to which year each Tekupha belongs. For if after the division a remainder of one is left, the Tekupha of Tishri is 9. 0; if two, the Tekupha of Tishri is 3. 0. And so on. When the hour is below 12, it falls in the night; when greater, in the day: and the 12 hours must be discarded. The Tekuphas alone are fixed and stable in the Jewish Computus, inasmuch as they are Solar. Temporibus Mosis Tekupha unica tantum dicebatur punctum interiectum inter finem anni antecedentis, & caput succedentis: idque in autumno: circa quod tempus quam proxime σκηνοπηγίαν celebrari praecipit Deus. Verbi gratia Tekuphae Tamuz solstitiali attribuunt ignem. Si enim post divisionem relinquatur unitas, Tekupha Tisri est 9. 0. si duo, Tekupha Tisri est 3. 0. Et ita deinceps.
