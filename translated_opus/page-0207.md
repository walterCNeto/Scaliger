For then the equal hours will be reckoned for the whole year from noon, which is the epoch [ἐν πινακικοῖς ψηφισμοῖς] "in tabular calculations" most customary among the ancient astronomers and the safest of all. Their cycle, however, does not differ from the old Alexandrian one except in its head or beginning, inasmuch as the Alexandrian cycle begins from the thirteenth, but the present one from the third. In the embolismic years they agree, so that the nearer Tishri falls in cycle 19, and the next-nearer in the eighth. Wherefore in those cases Passover preceded the vernal epoch both under the Seleucids, as long as they used the Alexandrian Callippic period, and in this Computation. For this reason it happened that in the eighth and nineteenth cycles they celebrated Passover twice in the same year. This is what Emperor Constantine touches upon in a certain letter to the bishops, as recorded by Eusebius; and it is repeated in the ancient Canons: [εἴ τις πρεσβύτερος, ἢ διάκονος τὴν ἁγίαν τοῦ Πάσχα ἡμέραν πρὸ τῆς ἐαρινῆς ἰσημερίας μετ' Ἰουδαίων ἐπιτελέσοι, καθαιρείσθω] "if any presbyter or deacon celebrates the holy day of Passover before the vernal equinox together with the Jews, let him be deposed". As to our saying that the hours are to be understood as unequal [πολιτικῶς] "civilly" in this computation, though [ἀστρονομικῶς] "astronomically" they are equinoctial, this also extends to the Tekuphoth themselves, which are reckoned by twelve hours, from the beginning of the night or day, although their calculations are supposed in equal hours. ### Notas do tradutor

Passagem sobre a sincronização entre o cômputo judaico moderno, o ciclo alexandrino antigo, e a Páscoa cristã. Scaliger discute a intercalação da Tekupha de Nisan e o problema da dupla celebração da Páscoa nos ciclos 8 e 19.
