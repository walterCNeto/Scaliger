For the Jews paid the greatest attention to the four quadrants of the day, and still do today, as will be shown in its proper place in the Jewish Computus. Therefore the Tekupha of Tisri fell on October 7; the Tekupha of Tebeth on January 7; the Tekupha of Nisan on April 8; the Tekupha of Thamuz on July 8: all on the letter G. Moreover, we have added here a small table of the characterism of the Jewish months, both in the common and in the embolismic year. But within each of the two kinds of year there are three further distinctions. For a year, whether common or embolismic, is either ordinary, or abundant, or defective. The ordinary common year has 354 days, the embolismic 384. They are called abundant and defective according as one day is added or subtracted. The seat of [ἐξαιρέσεως] "subtraction" is always in Caslev. For since Caslev is ordinarily full, extraordinarily it becomes hollow; and the place of [προσθέσεως] "addition" is in Marchesvan, which is ordinarily hollow and extraordinarily full. These differences arise from the scruple concerning the characterisms of Tisri and Nisan. For the characterism of Tisri can never fall on the first, fourth, or sixth weekday. The characterism of Nisan can never fall on the second, fourth, or sixth weekday. From this it is concluded that these characterisms apply only: for the new moon of Tisri, to weekdays 2, 3, 5, 7; for the new moon of Nisan, to 1, 3, 5, 7. From this it is again concluded that, when the character of Tisri is on the fifth weekday, the common year can never be defective, nor can the embolismic year be ordinary.
