on the tenth day of the waning month, on the sixth day of the waxing of Scirrhophoriōn, on the fourteenth of the Prytany of Scirrhophoriōn, 15 days before the magistracy of Pyrchiōn. For if, as I think, it fell on the fifteenth or sixteenth of the Metonic Scirrhophoriōn prytany, it would be distant only two days [ἀπὸ τροπῶν θερινῶν] "from the summer solstice." Here you see again how much it matters to know the ancient year of the Greeks, and to adjust the markings of times to its characters. For we, to whom no supports of antiquity are at hand, out of the most wretched remnants that lie scattered among the authors, investigate these matters and seize the fleeting time, and vie with the ancients, to whom all resources were available. Rightly, therefore, Plutarch [πανσελήνῳ] "at full moon," likewise [περὶ τροπὰς θερινάς] "about the summer solstice," because July 8 was only two days distant from the 16th of Metonic Scirrhophoriōn. The ancients, however, for the most part, assigned the full moon to the sixteenth rather than the fifteenth of the month. Had I wished to investigate by the true solstice of that time, which then fell on June 27, I should have labored in vain. For, as I have so often said, by "solstice" the ancients mean only July 8, and from the true solstice the full moon fell short not this year, but in the following year by three days. Livy says this event befell on the 15th day before the Kalends of Sextilis, which Servius likewise confirms in his seventh Commentary. The 18th day of Quintilis was 195 days from the Kalends of the old January. Therefore, at that time, the Kalends of January of the Romans fell on December 22, and already there was a defect in the intercalation of Mercedonius. But if the Alian disaster occurred on the 18th day of Quintilis, and at another time on the same day three hundred and three of the Fabii were slain, how then does Ovid place the disaster of the Fabii in February? Differently, Cornelius Tacitus, Histories II: "It was received as a sign of ill omen that Vitellius, having obtained the supreme pontificate, had issued an edict concerning ceremonies on the 16th day before the Kalends of August, anciently an unlucky day because of the Cremeran and Alian defeats." The ancients wrote ALIENSIS with a single L, not ALLIENSIS. Servius in his Commentary on Virgil, book seven: "Indeed it is called ALIA: to which a single L is added for the sake of the meter, as in RELLIQVIAS." Lucan put it well: "What Alian defeats...".

ON THE BATTLE OF LEUCTRA.

Eratosthenes places 61 completed years from the beginning of the Peloponnesian War to the Leuctrian defeat. The beginning of the Peloponnesian War is the year 4283 of the Julian period. Therefore, that defeat falls in the year 4344 of the Julian period, in Lunar cycle 12. It was thus either the second year of the 102nd Olympiad ending, or the third beginning. And indeed Plutarch says it happened [Ἱπποδρομίου μηνὸς πέμπτῃ ἱσταμένου] "on the fifth day of the waxing of the month Hippodromios," which month he equates with Hecatombaeon. Therefore, at the beginning of the third year of the 102nd Olympiad the battle of Leuctra occurred.
