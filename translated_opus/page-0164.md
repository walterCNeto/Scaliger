In the Table of neomenias in Julian years, the neomenia of Posideon falls on 8 December, the second weekday (Monday). Therefore on the fifteenth of Posideon, followed by the sixteenth, the Eclipse occurred. In the same year, both of Nabonassar and of Meton, the same luminary was eclipsed on 24 Pharmuthi, followed by the 25th, in the month of Skirrophorion. The time is 18 June, followed by the 19th, fifth weekday, followed by the sixth. The character 4 of the third period, combined with the character 2 of Skirrhophorion in year 12, gives the sixth weekday as the character of the neomenia of Skirrhophorion: which, since by the other table it falls on 4 June, fifth weekday, solar cycle 20, the Eclipse occurred again on the 15th of the month, followed by the 16th. Finally, in the year following of Nabonassar, and the thirteenth of the third Metonic period, the same luminary was eclipsed on 16 Thoth, followed by the 17th, according to the Athenians [Εὐκλείδου ἄρχοντος] "under the archonship of Euclides", in the month of the earlier Posideon. The time is 12 December, seventh weekday, followed by the first. The regular 4 composed with 4, casting out sevens, gives the first weekday for the neomenia of the earlier Posideon. Therefore the Moon was eclipsed on the 14th of the month, followed by the 15th, etc. Again, in the second year of the first Metonic cycle, at the outset of the Peloponnesian War, in summer days, [νουμηνίᾳ κατὰ σελήνην] "at the neomenia according to the Moon", as Thucydides says, the Sun was eclipsed. This eclipse occurred in the year 346 of Iphitus, 4283 of the Julian Period, on the third of August, fourth weekday, solar cycle 27, lunar 8, in the year 317 of Nabonassar, on 8 Pachon, one year and 37 days after the Solstice observed by Meton. ### Flags de incerteza

- Greek reconstructions of some accents and breathings in abbreviated forms (e.g. Ποσιδεῶνος προτέρου, ἱσταμένου) follow standard classical orthography; the printed text uses heavy ligatures.
- The Thucydidean quotation ἔσοσε appears unusual; possibly a typographical error for a form of the verb in the original Greek.


### Notas do tradutor

Page is dense with cross-checks between multiple calendrical systems (Julian, Nabonassar, Iphitus/Olympiad, Attic/Metonic, Jewish). Scaliger demonstrates concordance of two lunar eclipses and two solar eclipses (the last two drawn from Thucydides) across his Tables. 'Rursus' appears repeatedly as 'again/moreover' — never a proper name.
