on which falls the second year of Diocletian, that is, in the year of Christ 285, the Dionysian Paschal epact was 11, setting the new moon on September 16. But in the times of Justinian the new moon fell on September 15. Since, therefore, September is the first month of the Constantinopolitan year, an epact of 14 is required, which, combined with the unit, will set the new moon on September 15. In place of 11, then, we have taken 14, and for that reason, in place of the first cycle, the fourth cycle must be taken, whose epact is 14. Therefore 3 must not be subtracted from the Dionysian Paschal cycle, as in March, which is the beginning of the Roman ecclesiastical year, but on the contrary 3 must be added to the current cycle, in order to obtain the Constantinopolitan Lunar cycle. The year of the Constantinopolitan Paschal computation on which the Nativity of Christ falls is 5509, as was demonstrated in the preceding chapter. The first Dionysian Paschal cycle. The eighteenth Constantinopolitan. The Dionysian epact 11. It is required that the computation divided by 19 should leave 4 as remainder, for the Lunar cycle. But it leaves 18. Therefore, in order for it to leave 4, either 5 must be added or 14 subtracted. Hence 14 were subtracted from 5509, and 5495 remained, the first year of the Lunar epoch, on which the Nativity of Christ falls. Lest, therefore, they be compelled always to subtract 14 from the Paschal computation, they diminished the computation by 14 years in perpetuity, and thus they use a double epoch: the prior, which we called Paschal, and this, diminished by fourteen years, which we called Lunar, because it was devised for the Method of the Moon. Both they most ineptly reckon from the foundation of the world, and Cedrenus always uses this latter one, except in one or two places where, forgetful of himself, he uses the other in its stead; which also happened to Maximus the Monk. In the examples of Cedrenus, therefore, 5494 must always be subtracted from the current epoch, not 5508, in order to obtain the year of Christ, and that reckoning from the Kalends of September. For the Greek Church reckons the year of Christ four months earlier than the Latin Church. Other Greek authors of Constantinopolitan history also use this epoch. In this the Reader must proceed cautiously, lest he take one for the other. Otherwise a remarkable confusion in history would ensue. For the rest, this epoch provides only the method of the Moon in the age of Justinian, not however the method of the solar cycle and of the Indiction. in quem incidit secundus annus Diocletiani, id est, anno Christi 285, epacta Paschalis Dionysiana erat xi, statuens nouilunium in xvi Septembris. Sed temporibus Iustiniani nouilunium erat in xv. Quia igitur September est primus mensis anni Constantinopolitani, opus est epacta xiiii, quae cum vnitate composita statuet nouilunium in xv Septembris. Pro xi igitur assumpsimus xiiii, & ideo pro cyclo primo assumendus cyclus quartus, cuius epacta est xiiii. Non igitur iii detrahenda de cyclo Paschali Dionysiano, vt in Martio, qui est initium anni Ecclesiastici Romani, sed contra, iii cum currente cyclo componenda, vt habeas cyclum Lunarem Constantinopolitanum. Annus computi Paschalis Constantinopolitani, in quem incidit natalis Christi, est 5509, vt superiore capite demonstratum est. Cyclus Paschalis Dionysianus primus. Constantinopolitanus autem decimus octauus. Epacta Dionysiana xi. Oportet, vt computus diuisus per xix relinquat iiii, pro cyclo Lunari. Sed relinquit 18. Vt igitur relinquat iiii, aut v addenda, aut xiiii detrahenda. Igitur xiiii detracta sunt de 5509, & remanserunt 5495, annus primus epochae Lunaris, in quem Natalis Christi incidit.
