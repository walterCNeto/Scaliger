which was celebrated at the completion of every two years, with XXII and XXIII [days] alternately. If someone should object that, in Solinus, not XXII but XXI days were customarily intercalated, and that from this it must be concluded that the Greek year was of 360 days, and that at the end of the fourth year XXI days were added to prop up the epoch, we shall grant that this is true and holds for those nations which observed the Pentaeteric (five-year) festivals, not for those which had only the Trieteric (three-year) ones, in which the year was necessarily of 354 days, and XXII and XXIII days were intercalated alternately. After such intercalation the solemn Trieteric [festival] was restored. When, therefore, XXII days were intercalated, they marked that time by some notable rite, which they called Trietericon. But when XXIII days had been intercalated, they likewise marked this time with a more celebrated festival: as the Eleans with the Olympiad, the Phocians with the Pythiad, the Athenians with the Greater Quinquatria, and likewise with the Greater Mysteries. For they also had lesser [μυστήρια] "mysteries." Thus it came about that from four [years of] CCCLIIII days, and XLV intercalary [days], there arose 1461 days, which is the Solar lustrum of the Egyptians and Greeks, as Pliny also notes from Eudoxus. Just as it is certain that many nations of Greece, especially the Asiatic ones, used this intercalation, so it is not established whether the Eleans ever used it, or, if they at some time adopted it, when they ceased to use it. For their sacred games [πενταετηρικά] "pentaeteric" were always celebrated on the XVth day of the month, and indeed at the full moon. Therefore the month was purely Lunar. But their months were [τριακονθήμεροι] "of thirty days." And so, at the beginning of each five-year period, the month began from the new moon, as has been accurately discussed by us in Book One. The Greeks, therefore, consider as first the Olympiad which Iphitus, son of Praxonidas, instituted, in the year 3938 of the Julian period. For Censorinus, writing in the year 238 of the vulgar Christian era, which was the year 4951 of the Julian period, says that that year was the 1014th from the first Olympiad of Iphitus. Therefore the first Olympiad was celebrated in the year 3938 of the Julian period, on the 16th of Ab, July 23, feria III (Tuesday), whose neomenia had the character 3, 3. 9 0 5, on July 9, in the Jewish year 2985. Hence it came about that the neomenia of the first month of the Eleans, which for the Macedonians was Hyperberetaeus, was never advanced before July 9, with July 8 being set as its boundary and limit, which on that account they called [τροπὰς θερινάς] "summer solstices." It was the year of the world 3174. Neomenia of Zygon: 1. 16. 45, on the Kalends of October; Neomenia of Karkinon on the Kalends of July. ### Flags de incerteza

- 'Neomenia Zygonos: 1. 16. 45' — os valores numéricos (1, 16, 45) parecem ser coordenadas ou caracteres neomênicos; a pontuação e a forma exata podem variar.
- 'characterem habuit 3, 3. 9 0 5' — sequência de números cujo espaçamento no original é ambíguo; transcrito conforme aparece.
- Forma 'πρίοδος' transcrita do grego: provavelmente abreviação/forma tipográfica para περίοδος.
- Leitura 'Solomonis' (Salomão) conforme impresso; grafia latina usual seria 'Salomonis'.


### Notas do tradutor

Página trata da estrutura da octaeteris/pentaeteris grega e situa cronologicamente a primeira Olimpíada de Ífito no ano 3938 do Período Juliano, sincronizando com reinado de Uzias (Azarias) em Judá e com o ano 722 desde o Êxodo. Karkinon e Zygon são nomes de meses do zodíaco-calendário usado por Scaliger (correspondentes a Câncer e Libra).
