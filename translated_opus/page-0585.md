## English

now through Λ. that is LVSTRVM. ΛΟΥΣΤΡΟΝ. The beginning of Constantine the Great arises from the 8th day before the Kalends of October, in the year of Christ 307. Therefore his quinquennial celebrations coincide with the year 312. Again in the year 327, the same emperor gave the Vicennalia. From the Quinquennalia to the Vicennalia are exactly 15 years. Hence arose the practice of reckoning time by three Quinquennalia, or by Indictions, and the whole such period is called an Indiction, and its beginning is always from the 8th day before the Kalends of October. This period is noted in the Sicilian Fasti alongside Olympiad 273: Ὀλυμπ. σογ. Ἰνδικτιώνων Κωνσταντινιανῶν ἐντεῦθεν ἀρχή. Κωνστωτῖνυ Αυγ. τὸ γ, ἢ Λικυνίυ τὸ β ("Olympiad 273. From here the beginning of the Constantinian Indictions. In the 3rd consulship of Constantine Augustus and the 2nd of Licinius"). The first year of Olympiad 273 began at cycle X, in the year of Christ 313, during which that Indiction was running, namely from September 24 of the year 312. Therefore from September 24 four must always be added to the years of Christ, according to the method of the Indiction. But from the Kalends of January only three must be added. Accordingly, when four is added to 312, and all 15s are cast out, unity remains — namely the first year of the first Constantinian Indiction. I cannot, however, wonder enough at two most eminent men, Pantagathus and Onuphrius, the one of whom first linked this period to Constantine's victory over Maxentius on the 8th day before the Kalends of October in the year of Christ 313, and the other, persuaded by him, reported the same in his writings. But although it is true that Maxentius perished in the year of Christ 313, it is nevertheless false that this reckoning of time was instituted on account of that victory, since it is merely the Constantinian Quinquennalia; and it is false that the first Indiction began on the 8th day before the Kalends of October of the year 313, since it had already begun the year before. The Indiction, therefore, begins on the 8th day before the Kalends of October: a thing which the Roman Emperors always observed, and which the German Emperors observe to this day. This indeed can be unknown to no one, at least to anyone who cares to inspect present-day Imperial Diplomas. In the Theodosian Code, Book XI, Title XXVIII, the Emperors Honorius and Theodosius Augusti write thus: "in the 14th Indiction, in the Consulship of Valentinian Augustus III and Eutropius V.C." That was the year of Christ 370, in which, namely, Indiction XIII was running. But this Indiction XIV begins in September of the same year 370. Again, in a certain synod of Jerusalem: "After the Consulship of Flavius Belisarius most glorious, on the 13th day before the Kalends of October, in the 15th Indiction, in the Colony Aelia Metropolis, and in Jerusalem." And afterwards it is added, where those things are treated which had been done in the same year before the Kalends of September: "After the Consulship of Flavius Belisarius most illustrious, on the 6th day before the Nones of May, in the 14th Indiction." In the year of Christ 590, in the 9th Indiction, the epistles of Pope Gregory begin; and in the following February of the year 591, the same 9th Indiction is running — which is consistently maintained throughout all the books of the epistles. ### Flags de incerteza

- Transliteração grega 'Κωνστωτῖνυ' preservada como aparece no scan, embora provavelmente deva ler-se 'Κωνσταντίνου'; acentos e espíritos do grego impresso são aproximados
- A citação grega 'τὸ γ̄' (=terceiro) e 'τὸ β̄' (=segundo) tem mácrons/sobrescrições difíceis de representar
