## English

PROLEGOMENA.

How could anyone demonstrate it otherwise? From the creation of the world to the Exodus, 2452 years and six months have elapsed counting from autumn, or 2453 complete years counting from spring. But before the Exodus the beginning of the year was reckoned from autumn, and although that beginning was afterward transferred to the spring season, the tekuphah — that is, the end of the solar year — nonetheless remained in autumn, and it was around that tekuphah that God commanded [τὴν σκηνοπηγίαν] "the Feast of Tabernacles" to be celebrated. Therefore, where the beginning of the year lay from remotest antiquity, from there must also the beginning of things be reckoned. And this indeed we have done, rejecting the earlier opinion that placed the beginning of the world in spring. For the rest, consult the chapter on the creation of the world. Moreover, for those who use the lunar year, this beginning is more convenient and more in keeping with the tropical reckonings — starting from autumn rather than from spring — as is the case with the Jews on account of the [σκηνοπηγία] "Feast of Tabernacles" and the Passover. For if they were to adopt our celestial year, and took only this one precaution — that the earliest [σκηνοπηγία] "Feast of Tabernacles" should fall in the second Zygos — then the earliest Passover would always fall on the new moon of Krios; for the interval from the new moon of Zygos to the new moon of Krios is always 178 days, one day more than from the Feast of Tabernacles to Passover. We have already given the reasons for the Sabbatical year, and we have demonstrated that the true Sabbatical year has been observed by the Jews down to our own day, taking the beginning of the weeks not, of course, from the cessation of the manna — as certain fanatics and enemies of truth maintain — but from the 48th year of the Exodus, from chapter XIV of Joshua and according to the reasoning of the Hebrew doctors, who say that there were seven years of כבוש, that is, of the subjugation of the land, and seven of חלוק, that is, of its division. This is entirely correct: and therefore the first week to be counted is that of the division, not of the subjugation. Could there have been a Sabbatical year before the cultivation of the fields? It is madness to think otherwise. Nevertheless, there are and there will always be those who, out of sheer love of contradiction, in order to seem wise, will establish something different: to such people I grant leave not only to do this but even to mock us, since with them truth has no value at all. Whence arose the divergence concerning the epoch of the destruction of Ilium — some placing that disaster 407 years, others 405 years before the first Olympiad — we have explained from the doctrine of the Attic year, to which we owe whatever remarkable thing we have drawn out from deep oblivion. We have found the true opinion to be that of Eratosthenes, who assigns that calamity to the 407th year before the beginning of the first Olympiad: and we have shown its true day in the Julian year.
