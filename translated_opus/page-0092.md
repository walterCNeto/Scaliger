SOBRE OS MESES.

Dos dias formam-se [συστήματα καὶ ὁμάδες] "sistemas e agrupamentos", que constituem as notações e épocas dos tempos. O primeiro sistema (σύστημα) feito de dias chama-se Semana, coisa usada por todos os povos do Oriente desde a mais remota antiguidade, mas recebida por nós, europeus, apenas a muito custo após o cristianismo. Dela já se falou. Depois vem a [ὀγδοάς] "oitava" dos romanos, à qual sucedeu a nossa hebdômada. Pois a cada nono dia ocorriam as Nundinas, e esse espaço, no antigo calendário romano, era marcado com as letras de A a H, assim como no nosso calendário a hebdômada é marcada de A a G, inclusivamente, como se costuma dizer. Segue-se a [τρισκαιδεκάς] "tridecena" dos mexicanos. Pois o espaço que para nós se completa em sete dias, para eles se completa em treze. Assim, dos judeus é [ἑπταήμερον] "período de sete dias", dos antigos romanos [ὀκταήμερον] "período de oito dias", dos mexicanos [τρισκαιδεκαήμερον] "período de treze dias". O sistema (σύστημα) de dias mais próximo deste é o Mês, que se toma tanto naturalmente quanto civilmente. O mês natural é, ele mesmo, duplo. Pois é ou lunar, ou solar. ON THE MONTHS.

Out of days are formed [συστήματα καὶ ὁμάδες] "systems and groupings," which constitute the notations and epochs of times. The first σύστημα made from days is called the Week (Septimana), a thing used indeed by all the peoples of the East from the remotest antiquity, but received by us Europeans only at long last after Christianity. Of this we have already spoken. Next comes the Romans' [ὀγδοάς] "eight-day group," which was succeeded by our hebdomad. For on every ninth day the Nundinae were held, and that span was marked in the old Roman Calendar by the letters A through H, just as in our Calendar the Hebdomad is marked A through G inclusively, as they say. The Mexicans' [τρισκαιδεκάς] "thirteen-day group" follows. For what for us is completed in a span of seven days, for them is completed in thrice ten [thirteen]. Thus for the Jews it is [ἑπταήμερον] "a seven-day period," for the ancient Romans [ὀκταήμερον] "an eight-day period," for the Mexicans [τρισκαιδεκαήμερον] "a thirteen-day period." The next σύστημα of days after this is the Month, which is taken both naturally and civilly. The natural month is itself twofold. For it is either Lunar or Solar. The lunar is again of three kinds: either insofar as the Moon, setting out from the same point of the Zodiac, returns to the same point—this is called [περίπατος] "circuit," also [περίοδος σελήνης] "period of the Moon," an interval which is less than twenty-eight days but more than twenty-seven. The second kind is the return of the same body, having set out from the Sun, back to the same Sun. This is called [σύνοδος σελήνης] "conjunction of the Moon." The month of the third kind is the second day [ἀπὸ τῆς συνόδου] "from the conjunction," which is called [φάσις, φέγγιδιον[?], & ἀπόκρουσις σελήνης] "appearance, little gleam, and waning of the Moon." The second and third kinds have a place in civil reckonings. For the Athenians reckoned their new moons [ἀπὸ τῆς συνόδου] "from the conjunction"; today, however, the Hagarenes (Muslims) reckon them [ἀπὸ τῆς φάσεως] "from the appearance." DE MENSIBUS.

Ex diebus fiunt συστήματα καὶ ὁμάδες, quae notationes & epochas temporum constituunt. Primum σύστημα ex diebus dicitur Septimana, res omnibus quidem Orientis populis ab vltima vsque antiquitate vsitata, nobis autem Europaeis vix tandem post Christianismum recepta. De ea iam dictum est. Tum Romanorum ὀγδοάς: cui successit hebdomas nostra. Mexicanorum τρισκαιδεκάς sequitur. Quod enim spatium nobis septenis diebus, illis finitur ternis denis. Ita Iudaeorum est ἑπταήμερον, veterum Romanorum ὀκταήμερον, Mexicanorum τρισκαιδεκαήμερον.
