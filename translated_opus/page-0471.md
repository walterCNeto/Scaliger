to call a year one which had not yet begun. In Dionysius of Halicarnassus, the years reckoned from the founding of the City begin at the Catonian Palilia. Varro's authority had more followers than Cato's, who, as Censorinus says, "now comparing the times of various cities, now counting back their defects and intervals, dug out the truth and showed the light by which a certain number not only of years but also of days might be established." To this may be added Cicero's extensive praise: "You (Varro) have revealed to us the age of our country, the description of times, the laws of sacred rites, the discipline of priests, domestic and military affairs, the seats of regions and places, the names, kinds, functions, and causes of all divine and human matters." Velleius says that year 783 of the City began at the Palilia, in which fell the Consulship of Vinicius; and that the City began to be founded twenty-two full years after the first Olympic game, that is, in the twenty-third current Iphitean year. Censorinus says that the year of Christ 238 was the 1014th Iphitean year and the 991st of the City, reckoned from the Varronian Palilia. Plutarch likewise writes that the City began to be founded in the XXIII Iphitean year, and that on the day the first foundations were laid, the Sun was eclipsed. [ἐκείνῃ ᾗ τὴν ἡμέραν, ἐν ᾗ ὁ Ῥωμύλος τὴν πόλιν ἔκτισεν, ἀτρεκῆ τριακάδα τυχεῖν λέγουσι, καὶ σύνοδον ἐκλειπτικὴν ἐν αὐτῇ γενέσθαι (Ἑλήνης πρὸς ἥλιον, ἣν εἰδέναι ὁ Ἀντίμαχος οἶδεν) τὸν Τήιον ἐποποιὸν, ἔτι τρίτῳ τῆς ἕκτης Ὀλυμπιάδος συμπεσοῦσαν.] "On that day when Romulus founded the city, they say an exact thirtieth fell, and that an eclipse conjunction occurred on it (the one of Helena with the Sun, which Antimachus, the epic poet of Teos, claimed to know), coinciding with the third year of the sixth Olympiad." Cicero indeed mentions the conjunction of the luminaries in the second book of On Divination, but with no mention of a solar eclipse: "L. Tarrutius Firmanus, our intimate friend, especially learned in Chaldean calculations, also traced the birthday of our City back to the Palilia, on which he accepted that it had been founded by Romulus; and he said that Rome was born when the Moon was in conjunction, nor did he hesitate to sing its Destinies." If the Kalends of January of the ancient Roman year at that time began on the 8th of January — as it is most certain that at some point they began thence, this being the epoch of the ancient Attic year — then the twenty-sixth of Julian April would have coincided with the Palilia of the ancient year, with the Moon in conjunction, in the ninth lunar cycle: which agrees with Tarrutius's opinion, who places the birthday of the City on the twenty-first of Actian Pharmuthi, which is May 26, weekday two, new moon of Sivan 3008, whose character is 2.7.48.4. But it is clear that no solar eclipse could have occurred at that time, as appears from a solar eclipse which fell precisely 38 years later, in the following month, in the same cycle. For over the interval of a single cycle the failures of the luminaries may recur, but not over an interval of two cycles with one month more. From this one may gather with what judgment the writings of Solinus are to be read, who writes that L. ### Flags de incerteza

- Trecho grego de Plutarco: a leitura 'Ἑλήνης πρὸς ἥλιον' é incerta — pode tratar-se de corrupção textual (talvez 'σελήνης πρὸς ἥλιον', i.e. 'da Lua com o Sol'). Mantive a transliteração do impresso.
- A expressão 'ἣν εἰδέναι ὁ Ἀντίμαχος οἶδεν' é reconstrução da sequência impressa, que está pouco legível no scan (parece 'ἣν εἰδέναι ὁ Ἀντίμαχος οἶδεν' ou variante).
- Número 'DCCLXXXIII' (783) para o ano da Urbe confirmado por leitura dupla.
- Números 238, 1014, 991, 3008, 2.7.48.4, 38 confirmados por leitura dupla.


### Notas do tradutor

Página central da discussão de Scaliger sobre a cronologia da fundação de Roma, confrontando as tradições de Varrão, Catão, Censorino, Veleio, Plutarco, Cícero e Solino. O ponto astronômico crucial é a refutação do alegado eclipse solar na fundação de Roma: Scaliger demonstra por argumento de ciclos que tal eclipse seria astronomicamente impossível, usando a regra de que eclipses podem repetir-se num ciclo único, mas não em dois ciclos separados por um mês a mais.
